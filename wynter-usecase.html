What the Data Actually Says

"The headline is optimistic. The subtext is anxious."

The real read of Q1 2026

00 — Core tension
Spending more. Hitting less.
Teams are spending more on ads, hiring more people, and buying more software — while performance-to-plan sits at 49.0, still in contraction. This isn't confidence driving investment. It's pressure. The dominant behavior pattern is "invest our way out of a gap," not "we're winning and doubling down." That tension is the most important thing in this entire report.
66.2
VC-backed headcount expansion
Most aggressive hiring in the dataset — despite being the worst performers at 47.1 revenue plan. Investor growth mandates overriding execution reality.
35.7
Medium company (50-199) revenue performance
Deep contraction. Over half below plan. Yet they're also the most aggressive hirers at 65.7 and increasing software spend at 42.5%. Either a calculated bet or denial.
52.7
Mid-market (200-999) only segment above 50
The quiet winners. Best performance-to-plan, strong headcount expansion, leading agency spend. Scale without public-company constraints is proving to be an advantage.
01 — Software
AI isn't adding to the stack. It's replacing it.
49% of new software dollars going to AI and automation is huge. But the real signal is what's happening alongside it: 26% of teams are actively consolidating and cutting legacy tools. Seat-based enrichment tools, standalone ABM platforms, underused point solutions — being killed off and replaced by AI that does multiple jobs. For any martech company selling to marketers right now, this is existential. You either position around AI or you're in the cut column.
Replacement behavior
02 — Brand
Brand is back — and it's tied to AI discoverability.
Brand jumped from 39% to 45.5% as a top priority. But this isn't nostalgia. Several respondents explicitly tied brand investment to AI discoverability — meaning if your brand doesn't have a strong presence in AI-generated answers, you're invisible to a growing chunk of buyers. Brand is now infrastructure, not just awareness. This is the most forward-looking signal in the dataset.
Forward-looking signal
03 — Channels
Paid Search and LinkedIn hold. Everything else is getting cut.
The paid channel story is clean: Paid Search (42%) and LinkedIn (32%) are holding. Meta, YouTube, and programmatic are losing budget — cited for poor attribution and ROI. The organic story is more interesting: "AEO" (Answer Engine Optimization) now appears alongside SEO as a real budget category. Teams are already optimizing for AI-generated answers, not just Google rankings. 53.5% cite low ROI as the primary driver for cuts.
Channel reckoning
04 — Pipeline
Pipeline pressure is accelerating, not easing.
72% of respondents named pipeline, demand gen, or acquisition as a top-three priority — up from 66% in Q4 2025. This is not a recovery story. Teams are under more pressure than last quarter to produce pipeline, and they're throwing headcount, software, and ad spend at the problem. The question this data doesn't answer: is any of it working? Performance to plan at 49.0 says not yet.
High urgency

What This Data Doesn't Tell Us (And Should)

Gap 01
No NRR or retention data
Every investment signal is acquisition-focused. Is anyone actually growing through their existing base? Net revenue retention is the cleanest sign of product-market fit and it's completely absent.
Gap 02
AI priority vs. AI spend don't match
39.5% name AI as a top priority. But 49% of software dollars are already going there. Stated priority is lagging actual wallet behavior — suggesting either underreporting or that AI is now table stakes, not a priority.
Gap 03
No data on AI ROI measurement
Teams are betting nearly half their software budget on AI but there's no data on how they're measuring returns. This is the question everyone is quietly struggling with and it's a massive content opportunity.
Gap 04 → Opportunity
AEO is mentioned, not measured
Answer Engine Optimization shows up in investment data but isn't tracked as its own category yet. This gap is something Wynter could directly fill — and own — with a single added question next quarter.
